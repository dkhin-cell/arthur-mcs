# Decision 006 — Pricing Architecture: Free → Pro → Sentinel → Enterprise

**Date:** 2026-03-25 (initial), 2026-03-29 (finalized)  
**Status:** DECIDED  
**Agents Involved:** Claude (CTO Advisor) · Daniel (COO Advisor)  
**Director:** DK (Founder & Product Lead)  
**Outcome:** SPECCED — Pricing locked for launch

---

### Context

DK came into the pricing discussion with a prepared two-tier model and detailed revenue projections. This wasn't a "help me figure out pricing" conversation — DK had already done the competitive analysis, mapped Notion/Linear/Productboard price points, and built his initial model. He was pressure-testing with his CTO.

---

### DK's Initial Model and Adaptation

DK proposed Base at $29.99 and Pro at $49.99. Claude pushed back: "$29.99 competes on price with tools that do fundamentally different things. Consider $19.99 Base for accessibility, $49.99 Pro where the business lives." DK evaluated and accepted immediately: "19.99 Base and 49.99 Pro sounds good. Give me calculations based on 10,000 people signed up, 60% base, 40% pro."

DK then drove the revenue modeling himself — specifying the user split, requesting scaling scenarios from 500 to 25,000 users, and validating the gross margin calculation. He identified the Series A threshold (~3K users at $1.15M ARR) as the critical milestone to track.

As the product vision expanded toward Level 3 integrations, DK shaped the tier structure to match: Free (hook with Stage 0), Pro (individual PM power), Sentinel (organizational intelligence), Enterprise (custom). Each tier maps to a distinct value layer that DK defined based on his ICP analysis.

---

### The Decision

**What DK defined:**  
Four-tier structure with clear upgrade triggers at each level. Free → Pro when the PM needs more than Stage 0. Pro → Sentinel when the organization needs cross-mission intelligence. Sentinel → Enterprise when compliance and multi-workspace are required.

**DK's positioning line:**  
"Arthur doesn't compete with execution tools or storage tools. It competes with the PM's own judgment process. The question isn't 'is $49.99 worth it vs Notion?' — it's 'does this system make me a better PM?'"

---

### Lessons / Patterns

**DK comes prepared with a model, then adapts.** He didn't ask "what should we charge?" — he proposed a specific structure, stress-tested it with his CTO, accepted a better calibration, and drove the analysis forward. This is founder-level commercial thinking paired with intellectual flexibility.
